PVAMU: ROOTS AND RESILIENCE

    Prairie View A&M University, a historically black university, was founded during the Reconstruction era as the Alta Vista Agriculture and Mechanical College for Colored Youth. Its founding mission was to provide education to African Americans in the fields of agriculture, mechanics, and engineering, enabling them to contribute meaningfully to their communities and society at large.

    Throughout its history, Prairie View A&M has persevered through numerous challenges, including segregation and funding disparities. Despite these obstacles, the university has remained steadfast in its commitment to academic excellence and social progress.

    Today, Prairie View A&M University stands as a beacon of opportunity, offering a diverse range of programs and initiatives that empower students to excel in their chosen fields. From its humble beginnings to its present-day status as a comprehensive institution of higher learning, Prairie View A&M University continues to uphold its legacy of excellence and innovation, making it a symbol of resilience and academic achievement in the heart of Texas.

BACK IN TIME

    Established under the Morrill Act of 1862, which provided federal funding for land-grant colleges, Prairie View initially operated in a rented building with just two faculty members. Despite the limited resources, the institution quickly garnered support from the local community and prominent figures such as Booker T. Washington, who recognized the importance of education in advancing the rights and opportunities of African Americans.

    In 1887, under the leadership of President Edward L. Blackshear, the institution relocated to its current campus in Prairie View, Texas. This move marked a significant milestone in its development, providing a permanent home for academic pursuits and fostering a sense of community among students, faculty, and staff.

    Throughout its early years, Prairie View faced numerous challenges, including inadequate funding, segregation, and discrimination. Despite these obstacles, the institution remained steadfast in its commitment to providing quality education and expanding opportunities for African American students.

    By the turn of the 20th century, Prairie View had evolved into a thriving institution, offering a range of academic programs and becoming a cornerstone of higher education for African Americans in Texas and beyond. Its founding principles of resilience, perseverance, and academic excellence continue to shape the university's identity to this day.

Kendrea's Guide To Waller County: Exploring Resources and Recreation

Welcome to Waller, Texas, originally inhabited by the Tonkawa and Karankawa Indian tribes, was established on January 11th, 1884 by K.H. Faulkner. The town was named after Edwin Waller, the first judge of Waller County and one of the initial signers of the Texas Declaration of Independence.
